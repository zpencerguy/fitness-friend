# BellForge Competitive Analysis: Fitness App Landscape

Research date: June 15, 2026

## Executive Summary

- Fitbod is the closest threat. It offers AI generation with equipment awareness at approximately `$60/year`. Strong, but gym-centric.
- SmartWOD targets the same garage gym and coach programming niche, but lacks deep AI and is harder to find.
- Seconds is the benchmark for beautiful timers. BellForge's timer UX must match or beat it.
- Trainerize and Passion.io prove the influencer-branded model works, but at `$99-299/month` for trainers, they are not directly competing for end users.
- KBMH is a narrow niche that proves kettlebell-specific apps can charge freemium. It is not a major threat.
- No competitor combines equipment awareness, garage gym focus, modern UX, and affordable pricing. That is the gap.

## Competitor Deep Dives

## 1. SmartWOD

Website: `smartwod.app`

### Pricing

- Free tier: limited workouts, basic timer
- Paid: approximately `$5.99/month` or `$49.99/year`
- Frequent sales around `$29.99/year`
- One-time purchase option sometimes available

### Core Features

- Equipment-aware workout generation
- User selects available equipment, including rack, barbell, dumbbells, kettlebells, and more
- EMOM, Every Minute On the Minute, timer built in
- CrossFit-style workout programming
- Workout library with filters by equipment, time, and movement type
- Syncs with WatchOS app

### Equipment Awareness

Yes. This is a primary feature. The user checks boxes for what they have, and workouts filter to available gear.

### EMOM Timer

Yes. Native and well-built.

### Brand Model

Pure product brand. No influencer layer. Clean, functional, developer-driven feel.

### What SmartWOD Is Missing

- No AI generation. Workouts are pre-built or manually selected.
- No progressive programming, periodization, or adaptation to user history.
- Dated UX compared with Seconds.
- No barbell load calculator.
- No rep tracking.
- No analytics.
- No social or community layer.

## 2. Fitbod

Website: `fitbod.me`

### Pricing

- Free: 3 saved workouts, limited features
- Premium: approximately `$79.99/year`, frequently discounted to approximately `$59.99/year`
- iOS and watchOS apps

### Core Features

- AI-generated workout sessions based on muscle group targets
- Equipment awareness
- User enters available equipment, including barbell, dumbbells, machines, cables, and more
- Recovery and progression tracking
- Automatically adjusts volume and frequency
- Exercise library with video demos
- Works offline
- WatchOS companion

### Equipment Awareness

Yes. Fitbod asks what equipment the user has and generates workouts from available movements.

### EMOM Timer

No. Fitbod has no EMOM support. It uses standard rest timers only.

### Brand Model

Pure product brand. Well-funded, with Series A funding around 2023. Clean, functional, slightly premium positioning.

### What Fitbod Is Missing

- No EMOM or interval timer. This is a major gap for functional fitness users.
- No programming logic for structured periodization or workout-to-workout coherence.
- No barbell-specific features such as load calculator or percentage-based training.
- No community or social layer.
- Feels like a one-off workout generator instead of a structured program.

## 3. KBMH Kettlebell Workouts

### Pricing

- Free tier: limited workouts, basic timer
- Paid: approximately `$4.99/month` or `$39.99/year`
- One-time unlock sometimes available

### Core Features

- Kettlebell-only workout library
- EMOM timer included
- Simple, focused interface
- Timer presets for movements such as Turkish get-up and swings

### Equipment Awareness

Partial. It is kettlebell-focused only and does not work well beyond that niche. Single-tool limitation.

### EMOM Timer

Yes. Built in.

### Brand Model

Niche product. Possibly a single creator or small team.

### What KBMH Is Missing

- Too narrow. Only kettlebell users. Does not scale to a garage gym with multiple tools.
- No AI generation.
- No adaptive programming.
- No progression logic.
- Weak social and motivational features.

KBMH proves that niche fitness apps can charge freemium and survive. BellForge can do this for the garage gym category.

## 4. Seconds

Websites: `intervaltimer.com`, `seconds.app`

### Pricing

- Free: basic intervals, limited presets
- Pro: approximately `$9.99/year`
- One of the highest-rated fitness apps on iOS

### Core Features

- Beautiful, highly polished interval timer
- Tabata, EMOM, AMRAP, and custom intervals
- Sound design and haptic feedback are best in class
- Large library of community-built timers
- Siri shortcuts and Apple Watch complications
- Audio cues for transitions

### Equipment Awareness

No. It is a pure timer and has no workout content. It does not know or care what equipment the user has.

### EMOM Timer

Yes. One of the best implementations in the market.

### Brand Model

Pure product brand. Small team, very high quality, sustainable niche.

### What Seconds Is Missing

- No workout content or programming. It is a tool, not a program.
- No equipment awareness.
- No progression tracking.
- Does not know if the user got stronger.

Seconds is the UX benchmark. BellForge's timer must be this beautiful, then layer AI workout generation on top.

## 5. Trainerize and Passion.io

### Pricing

- Trainerize: approximately `$49-99/month` for trainers, varies by tier
- Passion.io: approximately `$49-99/month` for creators
- End users pay trainer-set subscription fees

### Core Features

- Trainer and coach-branded app delivery
- Workout programming, nutrition tracking, and habit coaching
- Client management, progress photos, and messaging
- White-label mobile apps for each trainer

### Equipment Awareness

No. Trainers decide what equipment to program. There is no native equipment awareness in the platform.

### EMOM Timer

Maybe. Some trainers use EMOM in their programming, but it is not a platform feature.

### Brand Model

Influencer and coach-branded. This is the model. Each trainer has their own branded app.

### What Trainerize and Passion.io Are Missing

- These platforms serve the coach-led model. BellForge serves the self-coached garage gym owner.
- No native AI workout generation. They rely entirely on coach content.
- Platform cost is for trainers, not end users.
- End-user pricing is set by each trainer and is inconsistent.

The influencer-branded model is proven. BellForge could offer a creator mode for popular garage gym coaches to white-label it.

## Strategic Recommendations

### Recommendation 1: Price Below Fitbod, Above Free

Position BellForge at `$8.99/month` or `$59.99/year`.

Fitbod charges `$60-80/year`. BellForge should undercut slightly while offering more: EMOM timer, equipment awareness, and AI. The free tier should be generous enough to build a habit, while locking progression tracking and export behind paid. Target: 10-15% cheaper, 30% more features.

### Recommendation 2: Beat SmartWOD on UX, Beat Seconds on Intelligence

SmartWOD has the equipment-aware angle but dated UX. Seconds is beautiful but does not understand workouts. BellForge should combine Seconds-level UX, SmartWOD's equipment logic, and Fitbod's AI. The timer must be as good as Seconds. The workout generation must feel smarter than SmartWOD.

### Recommendation 3: No Influencer Layer at Launch, Pure Product First

Trainerize and Passion.io prove the influencer model works, but it adds complexity and requires B2B sales infrastructure. Launch as a pure product brand. The creator and white-label angle is a Phase 2 play once BellForge has product-market fit. For now, own the self-coached garage gym position.

### Recommendation 4: Freemium Model Works

KBMH shows a niche fitness app can charge `$40/year` freemium and survive. BellForge has a broader niche: garage gym owners rather than kettlebell purists.

Free tier should include:

- Full timer: EMOM, intervals, Tabata
- Basic equipment setup, up to 5 pieces
- 10 AI workouts per month
- Basic tracking

Paid should add:

- Unlimited AI workouts
- Full equipment library
- Periodized programming
- Barbell load calculator
- Progress analytics
- Export and share

### Recommendation 5: The Real Gap Is Periodization

No competitor links workouts into a coherent program. Fitbod generates one session at a time. SmartWOD is a library. Seconds is a timer. None of them builds a 4-week program that progressively overloads and adapts.

If BellForge does periodized programming, where week 4 is harder than week 1 in a logical progression, that is the killer feature no one has.

## Key Findings Summary

- The equipment-aware plus AI generation combo is almost empty. Fitbod does AI, SmartWOD does equipment-aware well, and neither does EMOM well.
- EMOM timers are table stakes, not differentiators. They are built into SmartWOD, KBMH, and Seconds. BellForge needs it, but it is expected, not novel.
- The beautiful timer plus smart workout gap is real. Seconds has the UX, Fitbod has the AI, SmartWOD has the equipment logic. No one has all three.
- Garage gym owners are underserved. Most apps target gym members with machines and cables or CrossFit boxes. The person training in a garage with a barbell, rack, and kettlebells has no dedicated app.
- The price range is `$5-10/month` for fitness apps. Anything above `$15/month` needs strong justification such as coach relationships or elite content. BellForge's MVP is comfortably in the `$8.99-9.99/month` range.
